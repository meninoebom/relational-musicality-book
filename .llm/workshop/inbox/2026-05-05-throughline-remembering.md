# Where we're at — captured 2026-05-05

## The throughline: re-membering

The throughline of the book is remembering — specifically, re-membering in the literal sense of putting back together what gets dismembered.

Analytical thinking analyzes; conceptual thinking conceptualizes. Both slice. That's not a defect — it's their function, and it's how complex problems become tractable. But the slicing has a cost that accumulates: a person gets cut off from their own body, from other people, from whatever we might call source (not necessarily in a spiritual sense — in the very real sense of pre-conceptual contact with what's actually present).

So the practices the book is interested in aren't nostalgic gestures. They're a counter-process to something cognition does as its function. That keeps the framing structural rather than sentimental.

## The nesting (lens for the book)

Following John Vervaeke's term:

- **Ecologies of practice** — the genus. Practices that do remembering work.
- **Contemplative practices** — one species inside that genus.
- **Movement practices** — another species, the one Brandon is most interested in.
- **Choreo-musical rituals of the African diaspora** — the specific subset the book attends to.

The umbrella has to be wide enough to hold both contemplative (quiet, inward) and choreo-musical (loud, bodily, social) practices, because they're doing related work through different means.

## The political-philosophy facet

One thing the choreo-musical rituals do — among others — is theorize ideal human relations through their shape. The relations among players, between players and listeners, between bodies and rhythm, are a proposal about how human beings can be together.

Liberalism arose as one answer to the question: how must people live so that complex societies become livable? Christopher Small's musicking names a tradition that has been answering the same question, in different conditions, with different tools — for centuries, often by people for whom liberalism's answer was being denied.

So: musicking is, among other things, embodied political philosophy and social psychology. Not commentary on ideal human relations, but enacted form.

## Three faces of remembering (working sketch)

The practices reconnect at three levels:

- **Self to self** — body, presence, pre-conceptual contact.
- **Self to others** — the social shape of the ritual; the political-philosophy facet sits here.
- **Self to source** — kept agnostic; not a metaphysical claim.

## Provisional placement in the book

- Ecologies-of-practice nesting: seed early, probably Chapter 1 or 2, since it's the lens.
- Musicking-as-political-philosophy: natural home in Chapter 3 (The Worldview), where the embodied philosophy is already being described.
- Not the throughline. The throughline is remembering.

## Open questions

- Does Vervaeke's "ecologies of practice" want a named role in the book, or stay as background framing?
- Is the three-faces sketch (self/others/source) load-bearing structure, or scaffolding to discard?
- How to invoke the liberalism parallel without sliding into competitive framing ("liberalism failed, here's the better answer") — the move needs to stay closer to "another tradition working on the same problem."
