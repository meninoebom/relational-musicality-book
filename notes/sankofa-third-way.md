# The Sankofa Third Way

*A captured thought. Standalone for now. Possible home in* Relational Musicality*, possible home elsewhere.*

## The thought

Modernity is failing, or at least straining in ways that are getting hard to explain away. You do not have to accept the strongest version of that claim to feel the weight of the evidence: climate change, widening inequality, and the particular dangers that come bundled with globalization, the fast movement of disease, the faster movement of predatory capital. Something in the arrangement is not holding.

Two visions have stepped forward to answer the failure, and they are louder than anything in the middle.

The first looks backward and wants to return. Call it the reactionary vision. It reaches for a pre-modern order: conservative values, hierarchy, a strong leader, the nation as a body with a head. Its promise is that the fragmentation can be undone by going back to the form of society that existed before the thing that broke us. It treats the past as a place you can return to, intact, if you are willing to force it.

The second looks forward and wants to escape. Call it the accelerationist vision. It reaches past modernity into a post-human future: freedom from the body, minds uploaded to machines, bandwidth through neural links and satellites, technology moving fast enough to outrun the problems it created. Its promise is that the fragmentation can be transcended by leaving behind the thing that suffers it, the body, the place, the slow biological self. It treats the future as a rupture, a clean break from everything that came before.

Both are reactions to the same wound. One flees into the past, one flees into the future, and both flee the present, the body, the actual conditions we are standing in.

I am interested in a third way, and I think its template already exists. Not as a manifesto. As a practice, running for centuries, in the music of the African diaspora.

## Sankofa

Sankofa is an Akan concept, often rendered as *go back and get it*: you reach into the past to carry forward what you need. Its bird is turned backward while walking forward. The African diaspora built this shape into its music and it never stopped running.

Jazz is the clearest example. Jazz stays alive by looking back at what came before and innovating in relation to it. The innovation is never a rupture. It is not disruption in the Silicon Valley sense, the old thing broken and discarded so the new thing can take the market. It is a reinvigoration, a refreshing of a spirit that keeps the line to the ancestors unbroken. A jazz musician does something genuinely new precisely by being in deep conversation with what is already there: the standard, the tradition, the players before them. The new and the old are not in competition. The new is how the old stays alive, and the old is what gives the new its meaning.

This makes a shape. Not a line pointing back (the reactionary shape) and not a line pointing forward off a cliff (the accelerationist shape). A circle, or better, a spiral: the future and the past bound together, each feeding the other, motion that goes somewhere without ever severing the thread.

That is the third way I want to name. A response to the failure of modernity that neither returns to a frozen past nor ruptures into a disembodied future, but keeps moving forward by keeping the line back intact. Innovation as remembering. Progress that does not require amputation.

The diaspora refined this because it had to. When the line to the ancestors is under active attack, when the entire machinery of a civilization is built to sever it, keeping that line alive through musical practice is not nostalgia. It is survival, and it is a technology, and it turns out to be a design principle for how a culture can change without dying.

## Why this belongs near the book

The book already argues that these forms are wisdom traditions holding working answers to the hard problems of living together. This is the same claim aimed at a different problem: not "how do we live together" but "how does a civilization move through time without tearing itself apart, forward or backward."

And there is a sharper reason. The book takes centuries-old forms and proposes to build new tools from them, using AI, the very technology most identified with the accelerationist vision. That is a real tension, and Sankofa is the resolution the book has been circling without naming. What the book is doing is not extraction and it is not rupture. It is a Sankofa move: reaching back to carry something forward. The book is itself an instance of the third way it could be describing.

## Open questions

- Circle or spiral? A spiral admits real forward motion; a circle risks implying we only ever return to the same point. I lean spiral.
- Is this a coda to the existing book, a widening of stakes at the very end, or a thread woven through chapters 3, 6, and 7?
- The reactionary and accelerationist visions are political and named. The book so far critiques modernity at the level of the self and its tools, not at the level of political movements. Introducing named political poles changes the altitude. Is that a welcome widening or a different book?
